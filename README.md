<br>

Biotechnology wasn't the obvious choice. It isn't pre-med, it isn't pure engineering, and it doesn't come with a clear script for what happens after. I picked it because I wanted to understand how complex systems fail — where they crack, where they compensate, and what it actually takes to make them hold.

Three years into it at **HBTU**, I can say it delivered. Biotech is, at its core, a discipline of root causes. You don't treat the symptom; you trace the pathway. You don't patch the leak; you find where the design broke down. That kind of thinking turns out to be portable. I started noticing it everywhere — in broken processes, in products that almost work, in strategies that look clean on paper but fall apart at the edges. The lens doesn't care what industry you point it at.

So I stopped pointing it at just one.

Right now I'm reading about consulting frameworks in the morning and metaphysics by midnight. I'm thinking through how products get positioned, how biological data gets structured into something meaningful, how a single well-framed question can crack open a problem that seemed intractable. I haven't found the thing I want to do forever — and I'm increasingly unsure that's even the right thing to look for. What I do know is that the curiosity itself has never steered me wrong. Every rabbit hole has taught me something. Every field I've wandered into has sharpened the way I see the one I came from.

Maybe I'll land somewhere specific. Maybe the point is to stay exactly at this crossroads — where a biology degree meets a product instinct meets a genuine obsession with how ideas work. I don't know yet. But not-knowing, when it's honest and active and always reaching for the next thing to understand, is a pretty good place to be.
